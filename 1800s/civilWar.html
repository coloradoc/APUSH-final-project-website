The Civil War

The American Civil War, fought from 1861 to 1865, was an important turning point in American History arising from deep ideological divisions between Northern and Southern states. The Civil War was caused by debates between the Northern and Southern states over the spread of slavery into new territories. The war would change American policy over civil rights forever. The Union would win the war, leading to the abolition of slavery and a redefined relationship between the federal government and the states.

Causes of the Civil War

The Civil War was primarily caused by...

Click here to learn more

  • Slavery: One of the most influential causes to the civi war was the debate over the morality and legality of slavery. Since the country was first founded in 1776, slavery had been legal in the southern states, where it flourished into a major part of the southern economy. However, the same had not occured in the northern states, where the colder climate and rocky soil made plantation farming not viable. In addition, the Abolitionist movement began to grow in the North, which called for the end of slavery. With the Abolitionist movement expanding, many southerners felt that secession was the only way to protect slavery's institution.

  • The Election of 1860: Another event that influenced the South towards secession was the election of Abraham Lincoln in 1860. Earlier in American history, the northern population had outnumbered that of the South, which gave northerners control over the House of Representatives. Then in 1850, the South lost control of the Senate with the admission of California as a free state. With the election of Abraham Lincoln - a northern Republican - the North now had control over the 3 parts of the election process, allowing the North to pass laws without any support from the South. In addition, Abraham Lincoln was unheard of in the South, with many southern ballots not having him as an option. This infuriated many southerners and encouraged their decision to leave the Union.

  • States' Rights: As the powers of the federal government grew, debates continued over how much power the federal government should have. This can be seen in McCulloch v. Maryland, in which the supreme court ruled that federal laws take priority over state laws. Many southerners felt that since the states created the federal government, it only makes sense that they would have authority over the federal government. This is why when the southern states broke away from the Union, they formed a Confederacy, which is a form of government that puts emphasis on state governments.

Key Battles

  • The First Battle of Bull Run: This was one of the first battles in the Civil War. This battle was a Union defeat and showed both sides of the war that the war would be long and bloody.

  • The Battle of Antietam: This was one of the first battles that the confederates fought within the Union. The battle ended up being a Union victory, which pushed the confederates out of Maryland. This battle was important because it boosted Union moral and gave Abraham Lincoln the confidance to pass the Emancipation Proclamation.

  • The Battle of Gettysburg: This was another attempt by the confederates to win a battle in the Union. But again, the Union won the battle and pushed the confederates back. This battle is seen as being a turning point for the entire war, as it showed that the confederates could not capture D.C. and get a quick victory.

The Gettysburg Address is a famous short speech by President Abraham Lincoln, given during the Civil War in 1863. It happened at a cemetery dedication in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, a few months after a major battle took place there. This battle was significant as it marked a turning point in the war in favor of the Union forces, but it was also a tragic event with heavy casualties.

Lincoln's speech was short, only about two minutes, but its impact was huge. He touched on the ideas of equality from the Declaration of Independence and linked the war to the struggle for freedom and democracy. He also gave a powerful tribute to those who died at Gettysburg and asked everyone else to finish the work the fallen soldiers had started.

The speech is seen as a symbol of Lincoln's dedication to a united America and is a cornerstone of American history. Despite its brevity, it has left an indelible mark on the national consciousness.
