South Carolina

Founded in 1670

Panting of Charleston, South Carolina.

Originally, North Carolina and South Carolina were one colony (Carolina) before being split up into two colonies in 1712. In 1670, the first settlers, led by Sir John Colleton, settled in what would become the city of Charleston. Because of its southward position, the colony would become a major hub for using slavery to farm cash crops, such as rice and indigo.

Georgia

Founded in 1732

A painting of historic Savannah, the oldest city in Georgia.

Georgia was the last of the Thirteen Colonies to be established. Georgia was established by James Oglethrope in 1732. He wanted Georgia to serve as a refuge for those who are poor and in debt, as well as act as a buffer between English colonies and Spanish Colonies. The first settlers came to Georgia in 1733 and established the town of Savannah.

North Carolina

Founded in 1663

A recreation of a building in Bath, the oldest town in North Carolina.

In the 1650's, travelers from Virginia started venturing southward and established settlements below Virginia's southern border. Then in 1663, King Charles II granted a charter to establish a royal colony for lands below Virginia. This colony would be called Carolina (and eventually broken up into North and South Carolina). Like Virginia, North Carolina's economy relied heavily on farming tobacco.

Virginia

Founded in 1607

Recreation of the Jamestown settlement in the Colony of Virginia.

Virginia was one of the first English colonies to be established in North America. In 1607, the settlement of Jamestown was established in hopes of quickly finding gold. However, the unskilled settlers found none and were ill-prepared for the harsh living conditions. Despite this, the colony eventually flourished under the leadership of John Smith and John Rolfe. In 1619, Virginia introduced representative government to America with the establishment of the House of Burgesses, the first legislative house in the Thirteen Colonies.

Maryland

Founded in 1633

Recration of some buildings in St. Mary's City.

In 1633, Cecilius Calvert, also known as Lord Baltimore, founded Maryland with the vision of creating a haven for Catholics seeking refuge from religious persecution, particularly in England. This unique characteristic made Maryland one of the first colonies in promoting religious tolerance. Settlers arrived the following year in 1634 and established the town of St. Mary's City, which would eventually become the colony's capital. Similar to neighboring colonies, Maryland's economy flourished through tobacco farming, a practice that unfortunately relied on the use of slave labor.

Delaware

Founded in 1638

Old Swedes Church in Delaware, one of the oldest churches in America, built by the first Swedish settlers.

At first, the land that would become Delaware was under control of the Swedish. Then, the Dutch captured New Sweden and Delaware became part of the New Netherlands. Finally in 1664, the English captured New Netherlands and Delaware came under British control, where it would remain until becoming a part of the US. Originally, the land of Delaware was given to Pennsylvania. However, the inhabitants of Delaware wanted independence from Pennsylvania, and William Penn being the peace-loving Quaker that he was, he let them be independent.

New Jersey

Founded in 1664

A photo of a Quaker meeting house in Burlingtion, New Jersey created in 1678.

At first, New Jersey was under Dutch control as a part of New Amsterdam. Then in 1664, the English captured the Dutch settlement of New Amsterdam, renaming it New York, and also gained control of the land that would become New Jersey. The Duke of York then granted the land between the Hudson and Delaware Rivers to two noblemen, Lord John Berkeley and Sir George Carteret, who established the colony of New Jersey. The colony attracted settlers from various backgrounds and developed a economy based on farming and trading. Interestingly, New Jersey was divided into East Jersey and West Jersey in 1674, each having its own government. However, the colony would eventually reunite as a single royal colony in 1702.

Pennsylvania

Founded in 1682

A painting of historic Philadelphia, a major city in Pennsylvania's early history.

Pennsylvania was founded by William Penn in 1681. He was a Quaker, which was a newer sect of Christianity that focused on equality and peace. When he arrived in Pennsylvania, he structured the government around these principles, promoting religious tolerance and peaceful relations with Natives. He wanted to create a colony that his fellow Quakers could peacefully practice their religion in without experiencing persecution. With Dutch influence, the colony was quite diverse and had an economy based on farming and trading.

New York

Founded in 1624

A painting of The Castello Plan city map of Amsterdam, located in New York or New Amsterdam under Dutch rule.

New York was originally under the control of the Dutch, were it was referred to as "New Amsterdam." Then In 1664, the British seized control of New Amsterdam, renaming it New York in honor of the Duke of York. Because of its Dutch influence, New York became a very diverse colony and allured people of many different backgrounds.

Connecticut

Founded in 1636

Recration of the Parker House in Old Saybrook, Connecticut. Believed to be one of the first buildings built in Connecticut.

In 1633, English settlers from the Massachusetts Bay Colony started settling southward in areas that would become Connecticut. They later drafted a document known as "the Fundamental Orders," often considered the first written constitution in North America. This document established a democratic government for Connecticut. The colony attracted Puritan settlers seeking religious freedom and a more democratic society.

Rhode Island

Founded in 1636

Panting of Newport Harbor, a famous site in Rhode Island.

Rhode Island was founded in 1636 by Roger Williams (who is really awesome btw). After being expelled from the Massachusetts Bay Colony due to his dissenting views, Roger Williams established a settlement called Providence in what would later become Rhode Island. He argued for complete religious freedom and the separation of church and state. In 1644, the colony received a royal charter from King Charles II, formally recognizing it as a colony. In 1647, Rhode Island passed the first law in the American colonies guaranteeing religious freedom.

Massachusetts

Founded in 1630

Recration of a building in Plymouth, the site of the first Pilgrim settlement, founded in 1620.

Massachusetts, in its early days, was actually two distinct colonies that originated from separate backgrounds. The first was the well-known Plymouth Colony, established in 1620 by the Pilgrims seeking refuge from religious persecution. Then, in 1630, the Massachusetts Bay Company founded the Massachusetts Bay Colony, also populated by Puritans fleeing persecution in Britain. Both settlements were very religious and developed economies based on manufacturing, as the rocky soil made farming unviable. As the colonies expanded, they to unite, forming one colony known as the Massachusetts colony.

New Hampshire

Founded in 1623

Panting of Portsmouth, New Hampshire from across the Piscataqua River.

New Hampshire was founded in 1623 by Captain John Mason, who received a land grant to establish a fishing village called New Hampshire. The settlement faced numerous challenges, including conflicts with Native American tribes and economic struggles. In 1638, Massachusetts began exerting control over New Hampshire, incorporating it as a part of Massachusetts. However, in 1679, New Hampshire was separated from Massachusetts and became its own colony. The colony continued to grow through fishing, trade, and agriculture.
